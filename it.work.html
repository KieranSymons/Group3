IT Work – An Interview with an IT Professional

We sat down with Mr. Bobby Bazaad, a career IT professional with some serious credentials. He has worked across the board in his role as an IT project manager, business analyst and consultant. The conversation took place over a MS Teams video call and was led by Angadh Bazaad. Our goal was to gain an insight into the day-to-day world of an IT professional and get some realistic viewpoints on the internal machinations of an IT project. We all came away enthralled by Mr. Bazaad’s enthusiasm for the job, which he is evidently extremely accomplished at, and his willingness to pass on his wisdom to new generations of IT students. Below are the questions we raised to Mr. Bazaad and a summary of the important information we gleaned. A recording of the meeting can be found in Appendix C alongside a link to the transcript of the meeting.

Interview Questions: 
  1. Please tell us about your role as an IT Project Manager. What does your job entail and where do you spend most of your time? 
  2. Please tell us briefly about the industry you work in. 
  3. What are all the different people you work and interact with? Please elaborate on them. 
  4. As a Project manager have you ever interacted with clients or investors? 
  5. What aspects of being an IT Project Manager do you find most challenging? 
  6. In comparison, what aspects of being an IT Project Manager are most rewarding?
  7. What makes a great Project in your eyes? 
  8. What are some tools you use as an IT project manager? 
  9. What are some common mistakes you witness as an IT Project Manager? 
  10. Can you please share an example of work you do that best captures the essence of the IT Industry? 
  11. Can you give us a few examples of how the IT industry has evolved since you first entered it? (e.g., Tools, Apps, Procedures, Technologies)
  12. From your position as an IT professional, do you feel the industry is moving in a particular direction?
  13. What are your thoughts on the direction the industry is moving in? Will it impact your work within IT?
  14. Do you currently have any professional development plans to take you beyond your current role or into another sector of IT?
Interview Analysis 

What is the work done by the IT Professional? 
Mr. Bazaad has over 20 years' experience as program/delivery head where he has delivered large projects for Federal Government, Coca-Cola, Queensland Health, Rio Tinto, Transfield Services, Nestle, SAP, HP, EDS, OSR both government and private companies as per the defined project delivery frameworks. Mr. Bazaad has managed large programs directly impacting internal businesses and external agencies in a complex geographically dispersed business unit's system. During these roles Mr. Bazaad was responsible for planning business change and impact matrix, business benefit realization plans (BRP), change statements, change management plan and training for various stakeholders, vendors, and business end users. 

Successfully delivered complex programs including programs for: 

  • Coca-Cola, SAP TP project - $4.25 million
  • Queensland Health – SAP solution manager project - $1.75 million
  • Rio Tinto SAP CI projects – 16 projects valued at $10.65 million
  • Transfield Services SAP CI projects – 3 Projects valued at $500k
  • Nestle SAP and Global Application Managed Services - $15 million
  • Major field SAP Implementation for Saudi Arabia Airlines Worldwide - $34 million
  • Saudi Airlines Data, Transition, Transformation and Operations - $65 million

During these projects Mr. Bazaad was responsible for the overall success of software and product delivery, focusing on client relationship, fiscal management, project delivery, resourcing, reporting and overall project health. According to him it is important to have a client focused 'can-do' attitude and a strong focus on solutions rather than problems. For this role, an IT professional needs experience managing projects that combine understanding and employing multiple technology disciplines to deliver business outcomes faster. Project manager interacts and engages with clients in settings ranging from planning to delivery and maintenance.

Interactions with colleagues, management, other departments, and stakeholders. 

Effective management, leadership skills and positive team culture are important attributes to have in a successful project manager according to Mr. Bazaad. He has led & managed multi-skilled teams located geographically consisting Functional consultants, developers, support teams, business users & Admin staff.  Mr. Bazaad also led and managed teams in complex matrix structure in outsourced vendor environments. Mr. Bazaad was responsible for mentoring junior staff and managing vendors/suppliers' teams. To enable positive team culture, Mr. Bazaad gave his staff autonomy, authority, and responsibility to make their decisions. Mr. Bazaad was always there to support his team if required and had weekly meetings with staff to keep them informed and to ensure they feel part of the team with their colleagues. Mr. Bazaad mentioned it is essential to create open and positive dialogue within the team. Mr. Bazaad had mentioned one-to-one mentoring and counseling to address team member issues. Mr. Bazaad has done teams performance appraisals as per the company procedure when required. His past programs were delivered successfully due to his excellent program, communication and management skills which required an elevated level of personal drive to motivate teams and vendors. Mr. Bazaad always dealt internally and externally as per the corporate guidelines for all the deliveries despite adversity and uncertainty of new product and organization. His position required significant personal drive, integrity, and ability to work under pressure. 

Day in the life of an IT professional.

Most of Mr. Bazaad’s time is spent in Client-facing role leading end-to-end delivery lifecycle of complex, large-scale software and digital product solutions utilizing an Agile approach. Managing the project ideation, gathering the high-level business requirements, defining the detailed requirements and processes into epics and stories, Acting as Scrum Master overseeing daily stand-ups, sprint planning, demos, retrospectives these are the main things Mr. Bazaad spends his time on. More things include actively contributing to program increment planning, reporting and roadmap preparation. Mr. Bazaad also Manages client relationships and expectations as well as Communicating project status to all stakeholders (internal and external). Mr. Bazaad also mentioned he is constantly working to resolve issues and problems within projects and teams whilst proactively identifying project risks and making recommendations for resolution to ensure project success.

What aspects of the position are most challenging? 

We endeavored to find out what Mr. Bazaad felt was the most challenging aspect of his role as a project manager were. His answer was immediate, “the biggest mistake is, you know, when you don’t [stay] locked on the scope.” Mr. Bazaad felt that in his experience “salespeople … rush to sign off the deal, make it happen and if you missed the nitty gritty of that [deal] then you can get bogged down…, you don’t have clear requirements. You don’t have the clear scope. The goal post keeps moving.” This problem faced by many projects can cause issues to “the timeline, the budget, everything.” This is a significant insight especially with our group's ongoing work in these assignments, where we are attempting to provide a tangible project idea with associated artifacts, in assessment three. Limiting the scope of our ambitions will lend a big hand to our project. Thankfully, Mr. Bazaad supplied us with his trademark process for dealing with the issue of scope, a process he referred to as “the [four] W’s and H.”

“What needs to be delivered? Why we need to deliver it? Who’s [going] to deliver it? When it’s going to be delivered? And then how? So, these are the core. If you answer all these, you are the project manager.” 

Earlier in the interview in response to another question, Mr. Bazaad touched on another aspect of his various roles in project management he found rewarding and challenging. “There’s a human aspect, plays a big part in the project management”. What he is referring to is the challenges of working multi-nationally with many varying teams, and the challenges involved in overcoming “cultural background difference[s]” and “different linguistic skills”. Especially when it comes to motivation. Part of his earned experience is knowing how to get the best from people. Mr. Bazaad explained it like this. “Sometimes you gotta be hard, you know you gotta take out the whip as well. For people [who are not] performing but you need to … manage in a subtle way.” 

Where does IT industry seem to be moving?

Mr. Bazaad stated that he feels that “IT is always evolving. You know if somebody thinks whatever you learn now will last with you the rest of life then you’re kidding yourself.” He believes that “most of the new technological advancement is in artificial intelligence….” He points to examples such as the behavioural algorithms in place at social media networks and his own work in automation that will lead to the use of artificial intelligence as his evidence for this belief. We asked how he feels about this change and whether there will be any impact on his role as a project manager. In his usual quick manner, he let us in on his secret. “Someone has to implement it, right? Somebody has to code that. Someone has to test it.” Mr. Bazaad expanded on his point above about the need for ICT professionals to be constantly evolving with the technology, he admits “…I will have to upgrade myself with it.” And “Some other things underneath [change], like you know, but you just upskill yourself.” This was a common theme amongst the entire interview with Mr. Bazaad, he frequently emphasized how important it is that we, as prospective IT professionals, never let ourselves become obsolete, we should always be learning and upskilling with new and innovative technologies. 

“If I become stagnant then I won't be across the new technology, so I read through a lot of the technology papers that come out to read about what their technology is. When I go into the organization, I read about the, you know, the architecture there of the system, what is their read about the processes mapping and all that. So, these things you have to be to read it as a part of the project manager.” 

Advice for future IT professionals.

Mr. Bazaad had some insightful advice for future IT professionals, he is a firm believer that the industry is so wide, that there is a place for any and everyone. He gave this example; “You know you are being taught the basic. You're being given the base of the IT. You know you're being taught the fundamentals of programming as well. And what you'll find out is some of you may not have neck for the programming. OK, so that does not mean you got no career in IT. Not everyone who's doing it becomes a programmer.” He touched on several other important roles in the IT industry that are unrelated to pure programming such as a business analyst, “They are critical to the project because they are a bridge between a business and the right people.”, testers, project managers, support, and operations. Mr. Bazaad gave us some final advice on how to approach our education: 

“There's opportunities are endless, so go through your degree, see where your interest lies and then sort of move more into you know making yourself more expert into that… and see how you go.”

This sort of advice is extremely beneficial to beginners in the IT world as indeed, the scope of it is daunting, but with such large scope does come many possibilities, and confirmation that it can take time to find your niche is welcome.
